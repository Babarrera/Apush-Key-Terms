🔴 Period 1 (1491–1607)
1. Pre-Columbian Native Societies
📖 Definition / Details
Diverse, complex societies adapted to environments. Aztecs (Tenochtitlan, tribute), Pueblos (maize, cliff dwellings), Eastern Woodlands (matrilineal Iroquois, hunting/farming). Cahokia (30,000 people, 1200 CE). Maize cultivation → less hunting/gathering, permanent villages. Iroquois Confederacy. Animism.
⚡ Causes
Migration via Bering Strait; adaptation to regional environments.
📌 Effects
Population growth (Tenochtitlan 250,000); complex political structures; vulnerability to European diseases.
🔴 Period 1
2. Columbus's Voyage (1492)
📖 Definition
Triggered Columbian Exchange – global transfer of goods, people, diseases, ideas between Old and New Worlds.
⚡ Causes
European search for direct trade routes to Asia; Spanish sponsorship.
📌 Effects
Demographic catastrophe for Natives (80%+ died from disease); rise of transatlantic slave trade; European dominance.
🔴 Period 1
3. Columbian Exchange
📖 Definition
Exchange of animals, plants, microbes, people. Horses transformed Native mobility; corn/potatoes → European population boom; smallpox killed millions; tobacco fueled plantations; slavery created racial caste systems.
⚡ Causes
Contact between previously separate hemispheres after 1492.
📌 Effects
New World got horses, cattle, wheat, sugar, Old World diseases. Old World got corn, potatoes, tobacco, syphilis. Massive Native depopulation; African slavery expanded.
🔴 Period 1
4. Valladolid Debates (1550–1551)
📖 Definition
Sepúlveda: Indians are "natural slaves" – intellectually inferior, savage practices. Las Casas: Indians are brothers – sophisticated societies, capacity for reason, shared humanity.
⚡ Causes
Spanish brutality and forced conversion; religious/moral questions about conquest.
📌 Effects
No clear winner, but Las Casas helped shift some Spanish policies; set precedent for human rights discussions.
🔴 Period 1
5. Pueblo Revolt (1680)
📖 Definition
Successful Native uprising against Spanish in New Mexico. Destroyed churches, killed settlers, drove Spanish out for ~12 years.
⚡ Causes
Forced labor (encomienda), forced conversion, suppression of Pueblo religion.
📌 Effects
Spanish returned with more tolerance; became more cautious about forced conversion; symbol of Native resistance.
🔴 Period 1
6. Encomienda System
📖 Definition
Forced Native labor on plantations and mines. Spanish crown granted colonists right to extract labor/tribute in exchange for "protection" and Christian instruction.
⚡ Causes
Need for cheap labor after Columbus; Spanish desire to extract gold/silver.
📌 Effects
Massive Native population decline; replaced by African slave labor; creation of mestizo/mulatto classes.
🟠 Period 2 (1607–1754)
7. Jamestown Founded (1607)
📖 Definition
First permanent English settlement in North America. Initially disastrous (disease, starvation). Tobacco saved colony.
⚡ Causes
Joint-stock company (Virginia Company) seeking profit; English rivalry with Spain.
📌 Effects
Headright system; Bacon's Rebellion (1676) → shift from indentured servants to African slavery; House of Burgesses (1619) – first representative assembly.
🟠 Period 2
8. First Africans in Jamestown (1619)
📖 Definition
First recorded Africans arrive in English North America. Initially indentured servants, set stage for race-based chattel slavery.
⚡ Causes
Atlantic slave trade already established; demand for labor in tobacco fields.
📌 Effects
Gradual codification of chattel slavery (status of mother determined enslavement); rigid racial caste system.
🟠 Period 2
9. Mayflower Compact (1620)
📖 Definition
Agreement signed by Pilgrims before landing. Established self-government based on majority rule. First written framework of government in English colony.
⚡ Causes
Pilgrims landed outside Virginia Company's jurisdiction; needed social contract to maintain order.
📌 Effects
Model for later democratic governance; influenced New England town meetings.
🟠 Period 2
10. Massachusetts Bay / "City upon a Hill" (1630)
📖 Definition
Puritan colony led by John Winthrop. "City upon a hill" – model Christian society. Self-governance through freemen voting.
⚡ Causes
Religious persecution in England; desire to create reformed Protestant society.
📌 Effects
Religious intolerance (banished Roger Williams, Anne Hutchinson); town meeting government; foundation of New England identity.
🟠 Period 2
11. New Amsterdam (1624–1664)
📖 Definition
Dutch trading hub (later New York). Joint-stock company, religious toleration, fur trade alliances with Iroquois (guns).
⚡ Causes
Dutch desire for commercial profit; mercantilism.
📌 Effects
Became New York after English takeover (1664); legacy of religious and ethnic diversity.
🟠 Period 2
12. New France (1608)
📖 Definition
French colony centered on fur trade. Alliances and intermarriage with Natives (Métis). Few settlers, no representative assembly.
⚡ Causes
Fur trade (beaver pelts) highly profitable; French Catholic missions.
📌 Effects
Allied with Huron against Iroquois; limited colonial population; lost to Britain in 1763.
🟠 Period 2
13. Bacon's Rebellion (1676)
📖 Definition
Led by Nathaniel Bacon – frontier farmers (former indentured servants) attacked Natives and burned Jamestown, opposing Governor Berkeley's friendly Native policies.
⚡ Causes
Land scarcity for freed indentured servants; conflict with Natives; resentment toward coastal elite.
📌 Effects
Planters turned to African slaves as more controllable labor force. Exposed class tensions. Accelerated shift to race-based slavery.
🟠 Period 2
14. Metacom's War / King Philip's War (1675–76)
📖 Definition
Bloody conflict between New England colonists and Native coalition led by Metacom (King Philip). Devastated Native power in New England.
⚡ Causes
Colonial expansion onto Native lands; broken treaties; colonists' pressure on Native resources.
📌 Effects
Thousands dead; Native resistance crushed; New England colonists secured dominance; symbolized failure of coexistence.
🟠 Period 2
15. Salem Witch Trials (1692)
📖 Definition
Series of hearings and executions in colonial Massachusetts for alleged witchcraft. 20+ executed. Example of religious extremism and social anxiety.
⚡ Causes
Puritan religious intensity; social tensions; fear of outsiders/change.
📌 Effects
Decline of Puritan religious authority; shift toward Enlightenment thinking; caution against mass hysteria.
🟠 Period 2
16. Stono Rebellion (1739)
📖 Definition
Slave uprising in South Carolina. ~20 enslaved people gathered, killed several whites, attempted to flee to Spanish Florida.
⚡ Causes
Brutal chattel slavery conditions; Spanish promise of freedom to runaways.
📌 Effects
Stricter slave codes (Negro Act of 1740) – prohibited assembly, education, earning money; reduced enslaved autonomy.
🟠 Period 2
17. First Great Awakening (1730s–1770s)
📖 Definition
Religious revival emphasizing emotional, personal faith. Key figures: Jonathan Edwards, George Whitefield. Old Lights vs New Lights.
⚡ Causes
Reaction against dry, intellectual Enlightenment rationalism; desire for emotional religious experience.
📌 Effects
Undermined established church authority; encouraged individualism and questioning of authority; fostered shared colonial identity; increased denominations.
🟠 Period 2
18. Enlightenment
📖 Definition
Intellectual movement emphasizing reason, science, natural rights (John Locke: life, liberty, property). Social contract theory.
⚡ Causes
Scientific Revolution; European philosophers (Locke, Rousseau, Montesquieu).
📌 Effects
Philosophical foundation for American Revolution; undermined monarchy; influenced Declaration of Independence and Constitution.
🟠 Period 2
19. Navigation Acts (1651–1730s)
📖 Definition
British laws restricting colonial trade to English ships and enumerated goods. Mercantilism – colonies exist for mother country's benefit.
⚡ Causes
British desire to control colonial trade and maximize wealth.
📌 Effects
Limited colonial economic freedom; smuggling; salutary neglect allowed autonomy; after 1763 stricter enforcement sparked resistance.
🟡 Period 3 (1754–1800)
20. French and Indian War / Seven Years' War (1754–1763)
📖 Definition
Britain (+ colonists) vs. France (+ Indian allies) over Ohio River Valley. Treaty of Paris (1763): Britain gained French Canada & land east of Mississippi; France removed.
⚡ Causes
Competing claims to Ohio Valley; colonists wanted to expand west.
📌 Effects
British massive debt → end of salutary neglect → new taxes; Proclamation of 1763; Pontiac's Rebellion.
🟡 Period 3
21. Albany Plan of Union (1754)
📖 Definition
Benjamin Franklin's proposal for a unified colonial government (Grand Council) to manage defense and Indian affairs. Rejected.
⚡ Causes
Need for coordinated defense during French and Indian War.
📌 Effects
Precedent for later unification (1774 Continental Congress); "Join or Die" cartoon symbol of colonial unity.
🟡 Period 3
22. Proclamation of 1763
📖 Definition
Forbade colonial settlement west of Appalachians. Reserved land for Native Americans.
⚡ Causes
British desire to avoid further costly wars with Natives after Pontiac's Rebellion.
📌 Effects
Angered colonists who fought for western land; first major post-war grievance; many colonists ignored it.
🟡 Period 3
23. Sugar Act (1764)
📖 Definition
Tax on foreign molasses; expanded customs service; trials without juries (vice-admiralty courts).
⚡ Causes
British need to raise revenue after war debt.
📌 Effects
"No taxation without representation"; angered merchants.
🟡 Period 3
24. Stamp Act (1765)
📖 Definition
First direct tax on colonists – required stamps on printed goods. Internal tax.
⚡ Causes
British need for revenue.
📌 Effects
Violent protests, boycotts; Stamp Act Congress; Sons of Liberty; repealed but Declaratory Act passed.
🟡 Period 3
25. Townshend Acts (1767–1768)
📖 Definition
Taxes on imported goods (glass, lead, paper, tea). Revenue paid royal governors (making them independent of assemblies).
⚡ Causes
British need for revenue after Stamp Act repeal.
📌 Effects
Colonial boycotts; Daughters of Liberty; British troops sent to Boston (1768) → Boston Massacre (1770).
🟡 Period 3
26. Boston Massacre (1770)
📖 Definition
British soldiers fired into a crowd in Boston, killing five colonists. Propaganda used by Patriots (Paul Revere engraving).
⚡ Causes
Tensions from troop presence; colonists taunted soldiers.
📌 Effects
Increased anti-British sentiment; troops withdrawn from Boston.
🟡 Period 3
27. Tea Act (1773) & Boston Tea Party
📖 Definition
Tea Act gave British East India Company monopoly; actually lowered tea price but colonists saw as British control. Boston Tea Party – colonists dumped 342 chests of tea into harbor.
⚡ Causes
Company near bankruptcy; Parliament wanted to assert right to tax.
📌 Effects
Intolerable Acts (1774).
🟡 Period 3
28. Intolerable Acts (1774)
📖 Definition
British response to Boston Tea Party: closed Boston Harbor, revoked Massachusetts charter, allowed quartering of troops in private homes, allowed British officials to be tried in Britain.
⚡ Causes
Punish Massachusetts for Tea Party.
📌 Effects
First Continental Congress (1774); colonial unity; militias and Minutemen organized.
🟡 Period 3
29. First Continental Congress (1774)
📖 Definition
Delegates from 12 colonies met in Philadelphia. Issued Declaration of Rights and Grievances; agreed to boycott British goods; planned further resistance.
⚡ Causes
Intolerable Acts.
📌 Effects
Established colonial unity; called for Second Continental Congress; laid groundwork for revolution.
🟡 Period 3
30. Lexington and Concord (April 1775)
📖 Definition
"Shot heard round the world." British troops marched to seize colonial weapons; Minutemen met them; fighting began.
⚡ Causes
British attempt to disarm colonists; colonial militias prepared.
📌 Effects
Revolutionary War began; Second Continental Congress convened.
🟡 Period 3
31. Common Sense (January 1776)
📖 Definition
Thomas Paine's pamphlet attacking monarchy and hereditary rule. Argued it was "common sense" for a continent to not be ruled by a distant island. Sold 150,000+ copies.
⚡ Causes
Growing colonial resentment; Paine's radical ideas.
📌 Effects
Pivotal in turning public opinion toward independence; shifted debate from rights within British Empire to full separation.
🟡 Period 3
32. Declaration of Independence (July 4, 1776)
📖 Definition
Written by Thomas Jefferson, based on John Locke's natural rights (life, liberty, pursuit of happiness). List of grievances against King George III.
⚡ Causes
British tyranny; Enlightenment ideas; colonial unity after fighting began.
📌 Effects
Formalized revolution; inspired other movements; established American principles.
🟡 Period 3
33. Battle of Saratoga (1777)
📖 Definition
American victory over British in New York. Turning point of the war.
⚡ Causes
British strategy to cut off New England failed; American forces under Gates and Arnold.
📌 Effects
Convinced France to ally with America (Treaty of Alliance 1778); provided military/financial aid essential for victory.
🟡 Period 3
34. Articles of Confederation (Ratified 1781)
📖 Definition
First national government. Weak: no power to tax, no executive/judicial, 9/13 to pass laws, unanimous to amend. Accomplishments: won Revolutionary War, Northwest Ordinance (1787) banned slavery in NW territory.
⚡ Causes
Fear of strong central government like British monarchy.
📌 Effects
Shays' Rebellion (1786-87) exposed weakness → led to Constitutional Convention (1787).
🟡 Period 3
35. Shays' Rebellion (1786–1787)
📖 Definition
Massachusetts farmers (many Revolutionary War veterans) revolted over debt and foreclosures. Led by Daniel Shays. Marched on federal arsenal.
⚡ Causes
Post-war economic depression; high taxes; lack of paper money; AOC couldn't raise army.
📌 Effects
Exposed AOC weakness; proved need for stronger federal government; led directly to Constitutional Convention (1787).
🟡 Period 3
36. Constitutional Convention (1787)
📖 Definition
Great Compromise: House by population, Senate equal. 3/5 Compromise: enslaved counted as 3/5. Slave trade protected for 20 years. Fugitive Slave Clause.
⚡ Causes
AOC failures; Shays' Rebellion; need for stronger national government.
📌 Effects
Created U.S. Constitution (ratified 1788); Bill of Rights added 1791; separation of powers, checks and balances.
🟡 Period 3
37. Federalist Papers (1787–1788)
📖 Definition
85 essays by Hamilton, Madison, Jay urging ratification of Constitution. Explained and defended new government structure.
⚡ Causes
Anti-Federalist opposition to Constitution.
📌 Effects
Helped secure ratification; still used for constitutional interpretation.
🟡 Period 3
38. Anti-Federalists & Bill of Rights (1791)
📖 Definition
Anti-Federalists (Patrick Henry, George Mason) feared strong central government, demanded Bill of Rights. Bill of Rights = first 10 amendments.
⚡ Causes
Fear of tyranny like British monarchy.
📌 Effects
Bill of Rights added to secure ratification; limited federal power; protected individual liberties.
🟡 Period 3
39. Whiskey Rebellion (1794)
📖 Definition
Pennsylvania farmers protested federal excise tax on whiskey. Washington led ~13,000 federal troops to crush it.
⚡ Causes
Hamilton's whiskey tax; frontier farmers used whiskey as currency.
📌 Effects
Proved new federal government could enforce laws; showed strength under Constitution.
🟡 Period 3
40. Hamilton's Financial Plan (1790–1791)
📖 Definition
5 parts: assume state debts, create national debt, National Bank (BUS), whiskey tax, protective tariff.
⚡ Causes
Need to stabilize post-war economy; establish U.S. credit.
📌 Effects
Jefferson opposed (strict construction). Created first political parties: Federalists vs. Democratic-Republicans.
🟡 Period 3
41. Jay's Treaty (1795)
📖 Definition
British agreed to leave forts in Northwest Territory; no resolution on impressment or neutral rights; unpopular with France.
⚡ Causes
Ongoing tensions with Britain; need to avoid war.
📌 Effects
Angered France; further divided Federalists and Democratic-Republicans.
🟡 Period 3
42. Pinckney's Treaty (1795)
📖 Definition
Spain granted U.S. navigation rights on Mississippi River and right of deposit at New Orleans.
⚡ Causes
Spain feared U.S.-British alliance after Jay's Treaty.
📌 Effects
Opened western trade; reduced tensions with Spain.
🟡 Period 3
43. Washington's Farewell Address (1796)
📖 Definition
Warned against 1) political parties and 2) permanent foreign alliances.
⚡ Causes
Washington's two-term precedent; desire to guide future policy.
📌 Effects
Influenced U.S. isolationist policy for ~150 years; warning against parties proved prophetic.
🟡 Period 3
44. XYZ Affair (1797–1798)
📖 Definition
French officials demanded bribe ($250,000) just to meet with U.S. diplomats. "XYZ" replaced names of French agents.
⚡ Causes
French anger over Jay's Treaty; undeclared naval war (Quasi-War).
📌 Effects
Anti-French sentiment; Quasi-War with France (1798-1800); Alien and Sedition Acts.
🟡 Period 3
45. Alien and Sedition Acts (1798)
📖 Definition
Restricted immigrants (raised residency to 14 years, allowed deportation), criminalized criticism of government. Targeted Democratic-Republicans.
⚡ Causes
Federalist fear of foreign influence; French revolutionary radicalism.
📌 Effects
KY and VA Resolutions (Jefferson/Madison) argued nullification; backlash led to Jefferson's election (1800).
🟡 Period 3
46. Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions (1798–1799)
📖 Definition
Written secretly by Jefferson (KY) and Madison (VA). Argued states could nullify unconstitutional federal laws. Compact theory: Constitution = compact among states.
⚡ Causes
Alien and Sedition Acts; Federalist overreach.
📌 Effects
Precedent for states' rights arguments used later (nullification crisis, secession).
🟡 Period 3
47. Revolution of 1800 (Election of 1800)
📖 Definition
Jefferson (Democratic-Republican) defeated incumbent Adams (Federalist). First peaceful transfer of power between opposing parties in modern history.
⚡ Causes
Backlash against Alien and Sedition Acts; Federalist division.
📌 Effects
Established peaceful transition via election; marked decline of Federalists; led to 12th Amendment.
🟢 Period 4 (1800–1848)
48. Louisiana Purchase (1803)
📖 Definition
Jefferson bought from France for $15 million – doubled U.S. size. Jefferson used loose construction (ironic).
⚡ Causes
Napoleon needed money for European wars; U.S. wanted control of New Orleans.
📌 Effects
Opened vast western lands; fueled westward expansion; exacerbated slavery debate (Missouri Compromise later).
🟢 Period 4
49. Marbury v. Madison (1803)
📖 Definition
Supreme Court under John Marshall established judicial review – Court can declare laws unconstitutional.
⚡ Causes
Adams's "midnight judges"; Jefferson refused to deliver Marbury's commission.
📌 Effects
Established Supreme Court's power to strike down laws; cornerstone of judicial authority.
🟢 Period 4
50. Embargo Act (1807)
📖 Definition
Jefferson banned all foreign trade (especially with Britain/France) to pressure them to stop interfering with U.S. shipping.
⚡ Causes
Impressment of U.S. sailors; British/French violations of neutral rights.
📌 Effects
Crippled U.S. economy (exports fell 80%); massively unpopular; repealed 1809; contributed to War of 1812.
🟢 Period 4
51. War of 1812 (1812–1815)
📖 Definition
Fought over impressment, British forts in Northwest, Native conflicts (Tecumseh). Ended in stalemate; Treaty of Ghent restored pre-war boundaries.
⚡ Causes
British violations of neutral rights; war hawks (Henry Clay) wanted Canada; Native resistance (Tecumseh) aided by British.
📌 Effects
Increased nationalism; ended Federalist Party (Hartford Convention); spurred American manufacturing; "Era of Good Feelings."
🟢 Period 4
52. Tecumseh and Tenskwatawa (c. 1805–1813)
📖 Definition
Pan-Indian resistance movement. Tecumseh (war chief) and "The Prophet" (religious leader) united tribes against American expansion.
⚡ Causes
U.S. westward expansion; Treaty of Fort Wayne (1809) taking Indian lands.
📌 Effects
Allied with British in War of 1812; Tecumseh killed at Battle of Thames (1813); resistance collapsed.
🟢 Period 4
53. Monroe Doctrine (1823)
📖 Definition
President James Monroe warned European powers not to interfere in Western Hemisphere. U.S. would consider any colonization a hostile act.
⚡ Causes
Fear that European powers would reconquer Latin American republics.
📌 Effects
Cornerstone of U.S. foreign policy for 200 years; asserted U.S. dominance in Americas.
🟢 Period 4
54. Missouri Compromise (1820)
📖 Definition
Missouri admitted as slave state, Maine as free state. 36°30' line banned slavery north of it in Louisiana Territory (except Missouri).
⚡ Causes
Dispute over slavery expansion into Louisiana Purchase; need to maintain Senate balance.
📌 Effects
Temporarily resolved sectional conflict (lasted 34 years); postponed Civil War; precedent for using geography to limit slavery.
🟢 Period 4
55. Expansion of Democracy (1820s)
📖 Definition
Property requirements for voting eliminated for white men by 1820s. Fueled by Panic of 1819 and frontier states' example.
⚡ Causes
Jacksonian democracy; belief that ordinary white men should vote.
📌 Effects
Andrew Jackson elected (1828) as "Common Man" president; rise of Second Party System (Democrats vs. Whigs).
🟢 Period 4
56. Jackson's Presidency (1829–1837)
📖 Definition
"Common Man" president. Used spoils system ("rotation in office"), kitchen cabinet, aggressive veto. Democrats vs. Whigs.
⚡ Causes
Expansion of democracy; backlash against "corrupt bargain" (1824).
📌 Effects
Strengthened executive power; modern political party system.
🟢 Period 4
57. Indian Removal Act (1830)
📖 Definition
Forced Native American tribes to move west of Mississippi River. Led to Trail of Tears (1838): 16,000 Cherokee marched to Oklahoma; 4,000 died.
⚡ Causes
Desire for southern land (gold discovered in Georgia); Jackson's hostility to Natives.
📌 Effects
Cherokee removal despite Supreme Court ruling (Worcester v. Georgia, 1832 – Jackson ignored); devastated Native nations.
🟢 Period 4
58. Nullification Crisis (1832–1833)
📖 Definition
South Carolina declared Tariffs of 1828 and 1832 null and void. John C. Calhoun argued nullification. Jackson threatened force (Force Bill). Compromise tariff ended crisis.
⚡ Causes
Southern opposition to "Tariff of Abominations" (1828) which hurt southern economy.
📌 Effects
Showed federal supremacy; previewed secession argument (states' rights vs. federal power).
🟢 Period 4
59. Bank War (1832–1836)
📖 Definition
Jackson hated Second Bank of the U.S. (BUS) as "monster" for elites. Vetoed recharter, moved federal funds to "pet banks" (state banks).
⚡ Causes
Jackson's belief that BUS favored wealthy; Nicholas Biddle's early recharter bill.
📌 Effects
Panic of 1837 (severe depression) caused by pet banks' speculation; killed BUS; strengthened executive power.
🟢 Period 4
60. Second Great Awakening (c. 1790–1840s)
📖 Definition
Religious revival emphasizing human perfectibility and moral reform. Charles Finney – massive revivals in "Burned-Over District" (NY). Inspired reform movements.
⚡ Causes
Reaction against Enlightenment rationalism; desire for emotional religion.
📌 Effects
Inspired temperance, abolitionism, women's rights, education reform, mental health reform.
🟢 Period 4
61. Market Revolution
📖 Definition
Shift from subsistence economy to commercial/capitalist national economy. Transportation: Erie Canal, national road, steamboats, railroads. Communication: telegraph. Technology: interchangeable parts, cotton gin, steel plow, mechanical reaper.
⚡ Causes
Industrialization; innovation; government support (tariffs, internal improvements).
📌 Effects
Middle class (white-collar workers); Cult of Domesticity (women in home; men in public sphere); Irish/German immigration; wage labor; class tensions.
🟢 Period 4
62. Lowell Mill Girls (1820s–1840s)
📖 Definition
Young New England farm women worked in textile mills (Lowell, MA). Lived in company boarding houses; experienced clock-regulated work.
⚡ Causes
Market Revolution; need for factory labor; farm families needed cash.
📌 Effects
Early labor organizing (Lowell Female Labor Reform Association); but working conditions declined over time.
🟢 Period 4
63. Irish Immigration (1820s–1840s)
📖 Definition
Potato Famine (1845-52) drove 1+ million Irish to U.S. Settled in cities; low-wage labor; faced anti-Catholic nativism.
⚡ Causes
Famine; British oppression; economic opportunity in U.S.
📌 Effects
Nativist backlash (Know-Nothing Party); provided labor for canals, railroads, factories; influenced urban politics.
🟢 Period 4
64. German Immigration (1820s–1840s)
📖 Definition
Failed 1848 revolutions and economic reasons. Moved west (Midwest farms); more skilled than Irish; opposed slavery.
⚡ Causes
Political unrest in German states; economic opportunity.
📌 Effects
Contributed to farming and industry; stronger anti-slavery sentiment in Midwest; supported public education.
🟢 Period 4
65. Nativism / Know-Nothing Party (1840s–1850s)
📖 Definition
Anti-immigrant, anti-Catholic movement. American Party (Know-Nothings) feared immigrants corrupted elections and took jobs.
⚡ Causes
Surge of Irish and German immigration; Protestant fear of Catholic influence.
📌 Effects
Political violence; restrictions on immigrants; Know-Nothings briefly powerful (1850s) before splitting over slavery.
🟢 Period 4
66. Abolitionist Movement (1830s–1860s)
📖 Definition
Movement to end slavery. Key figures: William Lloyd Garrison (The Liberator, immediate emancipation), Frederick Douglass (North Star), Grimké sisters. Nat Turner's Rebellion (1831) killed ~60 whites → harsher slave codes.
⚡ Causes
Second Great Awakening moral reform; Enlightenment ideals; slave resistance.
📌 Effects
Southern reaction: "positive good" argument; Gag Rule (1836-1844) tabled antislavery petitions; deepened sectional divide.
🟢 Period 4
67. Nat Turner's Rebellion (1831)
📖 Definition
Virginia slave revolt led by Nat Turner. Rebels killed ~60 whites before suppressed. Turner executed.
⚡ Causes
Religious vision; brutal slavery conditions.
📌 Effects
Harsher slave codes (prohibited teaching slaves to read, assembly, preaching); increased Southern fear; inspired abolitionists.
🟢 Period 4
68. Seneca Falls Convention (1848)
📖 Definition
First women's rights convention. Issued Declaration of Sentiments (modeled on Declaration of Independence) demanding full equality, including woman suffrage. Led by Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Lucretia Mott.
⚡ Causes
Women's experience in abolitionist movement (excluded at 1840 World Anti-Slavery Convention); Second Great Awakening.
📌 Effects
Launched women's suffrage movement; 72 years later → 19th Amendment (1920).
🟢 Period 4
69. Dorothea Dix (1820s–1850s)
📖 Definition
Mental health and prison reformer. Documented horrific conditions for mentally ill and prisoners; lobbied state legislatures.
⚡ Causes
Second Great Awakening humanitarian impulses.
📌 Effects
Created first generation of mental asylums; improved prison conditions.
🟢 Period 4
70. Horace Mann (1830s–1840s)
📖 Definition
"Father of American Education." Advocated for public, free, non-sectarian common schools.
⚡ Causes
Belief that democracy required educated citizens; reform spirit.
📌 Effects
Established public school systems (Massachusetts model spread nationwide); teacher training (normal schools).
🟢 Period 4
71. Cult of Domesticity / Republican Motherhood (early 1800s)
📖 Definition
Ideal woman = pious, pure, submissive, in the home (private sphere). Men = public sphere. Women's role to raise virtuous, educated sons for the republic.
⚡ Causes
Market Revolution separated work/home; earlier Republican Motherhood (post-Revolution).
📌 Effects
Limited women to domestic sphere but paradoxically elevated education for women (to teach sons). Contradicted by later feminism.
🔵 Period 5 (1844–1877)
72. Manifest Destiny (1845)
📖 Definition
Belief coined by John L. O'Sullivan: God-given right to expand across continent. Used to justify war and land acquisition.
⚡ Causes
American exceptionalism; economic ambition; fear of European powers.
📌 Effects
Texas annexation (1845); Mexican-American War (1846-48); forced Indian removal; expansion of slavery debates.
🔵 Period 5
73. Texas Annexation (1845)
📖 Definition
U.S. annexed Republic of Texas as slave state. Angered Mexico; took nearly 10 years due to fears of war and Northern backlash.
⚡ Causes
Texas independent from Mexico (1836); Southern desire for slave state expansion.
📌 Effects
Mexican-American War; deepened sectional divide.
🔵 Period 5
74. Mexican-American War (1846–1848)
📖 Definition
U.S. victory; Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo gave Mexican Cession (CA, NM, AZ, UT, NV). U.S. paid $15 million.
⚡ Causes
Border dispute (Nueces River vs. Rio Grande); President Polk provoked Mexico; Manifest Destiny.
📌 Effects
Massive land acquisition; reignited slavery debate (Wilmot Proviso); Abraham Lincoln criticized war; Thoreau wrote "Civil Disobedience."
🔵 Period 5
75. Wilmot Proviso (1846)
📖 Definition
Proposed banning slavery in ALL territory acquired from Mexico. Passed House twice, failed in Senate.
⚡ Causes
Northern fear of slave power expansion; Free Soil ideology.
📌 Effects
Terrified the South; showed Northern determination to stop slavery; set stage for 1850 Compromise.
🔵 Period 5
76. Compromise of 1850
📖 Definition
1) CA free state; 2) Popular sovereignty in UT/NM; 3) Stricter Fugitive Slave Law; 4) Ended DC slave trade.
⚡ Causes
Debate over slavery in Mexican Cession; Gold Rush (CA applied as free state).
📌 Effects
Fugitive Slave Law backfired – radicalized Northerners forced to catch runaways. Temporarily delayed secession.
🔵 Period 5
77. Fugitive Slave Law (1850)
📖 Definition
Part of Compromise of 1850. Required citizens to help capture runaway slaves; denied alleged fugitives trial by jury; federal commissioners paid more for returning than freeing.
⚡ Causes
Southern demand for enforcement of constitutional fugitive slave clause.
📌 Effects
Northerners forced to participate in slavery; increased Underground Railroad activity (Harriet Tubman); radicalized moderate Northerners; led to personal liberty laws.
🔵 Period 5
78. Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854)
📖 Definition
Applied popular sovereignty to KS and NB territories, repealing the Missouri Compromise line (36°30'). THE TURNING POINT.
⚡ Causes
Stephen Douglas wanted transcontinental railroad through Chicago; needed Southern support.
📌 Effects
"Bleeding Kansas" – pro-slavery "Border Ruffians" vs. anti-slavery "Free-Soilers" fought. Destroyed Whig Party; created Republican Party (anti-slavery expansion). Paved way for Civil War.
🔵 Period 5
79. Bleeding Kansas (1855–1859)
📖 Definition
Violent conflict over slavery in Kansas. Events: Sack of Lawrence (pro-slavery attack); John Brown's Pottawatomie Creek Massacre (anti-slavery revenge - 5 killed).
⚡ Causes
Kansas-Nebraska Act; popular sovereignty; both sides sent settlers/arms.
📌 Effects
Over 150 killed; previewed Civil War violence; radicalized John Brown.
🔵 Period 5
80. Caning of Charles Sumner (1856)
📖 Definition
Senator Charles Sumner (MA) gave anti-slavery speech insulting Senator Butler (SC). Butler's nephew, Rep. Preston Brooks, beat Sumner unconscious with a cane on Senate floor.
⚡ Causes
Sectional hatred; Southern honor culture.
📌 Effects
Sumner became martyr in North; Brooks hailed as hero in South. Symbolized breakdown of civility.
🔵 Period 5
81. Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857)
📖 Definition
Supreme Court ruled: 1) Blacks (free or enslaved) were not citizens; 2) Slaves were property; 3) Congress could not ban slavery in territories (Missouri Compromise unconstitutional).
⚡ Causes
Dred Scott sued for freedom after living in free territories; Chief Justice Taney (pro-slavery).
📌 Effects
Outraged the North; declared popular sovereignty unconstitutional; made slavery legal in all territories; pushed nation closer to war.
🔵 Period 5
82. Lincoln-Douglas Debates (1858)
📖 Definition
Senate debates in Illinois. Lincoln's "House Divided" speech ("a house divided against itself cannot stand"). Douglas's "Freeport Doctrine" – territories could ban slavery by refusing to pass laws protecting it.
⚡ Causes
Both men seeking Senate seat (Douglas won).
📌 Effects
Made Lincoln nationally known; Freeport Doctrine alienated Southern Democrats (they would nominate Breckinridge in 1860).
🔵 Period 5
83. John Brown's Raid on Harpers Ferry (1859)
📖 Definition
Brown led small group to seize federal arsenal, hoping to incite slave rebellion. Failed; Brown captured and executed.
⚡ Causes
Brown's radical abolitionism; belief that only violence would end slavery.
📌 Effects
Brown became martyr in North; demon in the South (convinced them North wanted violent revolution). Final tipping point.
🔵 Period 5
84. Election of 1860
📖 Definition
Abraham Lincoln (Republican) won without any Southern electoral votes. Free-soil platform (no extension of slavery).
⚡ Causes
Democratic Party split – Northern (Douglas) vs. Southern (Breckinridge); Constitutional Union (Bell).
📌 Effects
South Carolina seceded Dec 1860; 7 states formed Confederate States of America before Lincoln took office. Civil War imminent.
🔵 Period 5
85. Fort Sumter (April 1861)
📖 Definition
Confederate forces attacked U.S. fort in Charleston harbor. U.S. surrendered after 34 hours. First battle of Civil War.
⚡ Causes
Lincoln refused to surrender fort; sent supply ships; Confederates saw as aggression.
📌 Effects
Unites the North; brings 4 more states (VA, NC, TN, AR) to Confederacy. Lincoln calls for 75,000 volunteers.
🔵 Period 5
86. Emancipation Proclamation (January 1, 1863)
📖 Definition
Freed slaves ONLY in areas of rebellion (not Border States or conquered areas). Changed war goal from preserving Union to ending slavery.
⚡ Causes
Military necessity (weaken South); keep Europe (esp. Britain) from aiding Confederacy.
📌 Effects
Allowed Black enlistment (180,000+ served, e.g., 54th Massachusetts). Turned Civil War into moral crusade. Didn't free all slaves but paved way for 13th Amendment.
🔵 Period 5
87. Battle of Gettysburg (July 1863)
📖 Definition
Union (Meade) defeated Lee's invasion of Pennsylvania. Bloodiest battle (~51,000 casualties). Lincoln's Gettysburg Address redefined war as preserving democracy.
⚡ Causes
Lee wanted to threaten Northern cities, force peace, gain European recognition.
📌 Effects
Stopped Lee's invasion; Confederacy never recovered; Union morale boosted.
🔵 Period 5
88. Battle of Vicksburg (July 1863)
📖 Definition
Union (Grant) captured Vicksburg, Mississippi. Last Confederate stronghold on Mississippi River.
⚡ Causes
Anaconda Plan – control Mississippi to split South.
📌 Effects
Union controlled entire Mississippi River; split Confederacy in two; Grant promoted to overall commander.
🔵 Period 5
89. Sherman's March to the Sea (1864)
📖 Definition
General William Tecumseh Sherman led 60,000 troops from Atlanta to Savannah, destroying railroads, factories, farms, and cities ("total war").
⚡ Causes
Demoralize South; destroy ability to fight; break civilian support for war.
📌 Effects
Devastated Georgia; shortened war by destroying Confederate resources; "scorched earth" became model for modern warfare.
🔵 Period 5
90. Appomattox (April 9, 1865)
📖 Definition
Lee surrendered to Grant at Appomattox Court House, Virginia. Grant offered generous terms (let soldiers keep horses, officers keep sidearms).
⚡ Causes
Confederate army surrounded, outnumbered, starving.
📌 Effects
Civil War ended. Reconstruction began. Lincoln assassinated 5 days later (April 14, 1865).
🔵 Period 5
91. 13th Amendment (1865)
📖 Definition
Abolished slavery throughout the United States.
⚡ Causes
Civil War; Emancipation Proclamation (which was war measure, not constitutional amendment).
📌 Effects
Ended legal slavery (but allowed involuntary servitude as punishment for crime – exception used for convict leasing). Required further amendments to secure rights.
🔵 Period 5
92. Reconstruction (1865–1877)
📖 Definition
Period of rebuilding South and integrating freed people. Three plans: Lincoln's 10% Plan (lenient), Johnson's Plan (very lenient), Congressional/Radical Reconstruction (harsh – 5 military districts, black male suffrage, 14th Amendment required).
⚡ Causes
Need to readmit Southern states; determine status of 4 million freed people.
📌 Effects
14th and 15th Amendments; Freedmen's Bureau; Black Codes; sharecropping; Klan violence; ended by Compromise of 1877.
🔵 Period 5
93. Freedmen's Bureau (1865)
📖 Definition
Federal agency provided food, housing, medical care, legal help, and schools to freed people and poor whites.
⚡ Causes
Need to assist transition from slavery to freedom.
📌 Effects
Established over 1,000 schools (including Howard, Fisk, Morehouse); helped reunite families; opposed by Southern whites; dismantled 1872.
🔵 Period 5
94. Black Codes (1865–1866)
📖 Definition
Southern laws restricting freedmen's movement and forcing them into labor contracts (often with former masters). Outraged the North.
⚡ Causes
Southern whites wanted to maintain racial control and cheap labor.
📌 Effects
Radical Republicans passed Civil Rights Act of 1866 and 14th Amendment to override Black Codes; Johnson vetoed; Congress overrode.
🔵 Period 5
95. 14th Amendment (1868)
📖 Definition
Defined national citizenship; guaranteed equal protection under law and due process; penalized states that denied suffrage (reduced representation).
⚡ Causes
Need to protect freed people from Black Codes; overturn Dred Scott.
📌 Effects
Provided constitutional basis for civil rights (later used for school desegregation, marriage equality, etc.). Required for Southern readmission.
🔵 Period 5
96. 15th Amendment (1870)
📖 Definition
Prohibited denying vote based on race, color, or previous condition of servitude (but allowed literacy tests, poll taxes, grandfather clauses).
⚡ Causes
Republican desire to protect black suffrage in South; ensure Republican votes.
📌 Effects
Black men voted in large numbers (Reconstruction legislatures); later nullified by Jim Crow laws and violence.
🔵 Period 5
97. Sharecropping
📖 Definition
System where families farmed land in exchange for share of crop (usually 1/2 to landowner). Landowners provided seed, tools, housing. Led to perpetual debt.
⚡ Causes
End of slavery; no land redistribution; freed people needed work; planters needed labor.
📌 Effects
Replaced slavery with economic dependency; kept Black Southerners in poverty; enforced through crop liens and debt peonage.
🔵 Period 5
98. Ku Klux Klan (Reconstruction)
📖 Definition
White supremacist terrorist group used violence (murder, whipping, burning) to suppress Black voting and restore white Democratic rule.
⚡ Causes
White resentment of Reconstruction; black political participation.
📌 Effects
Intimidated Black voters; led to Enforcement Acts (1870-71) and federal troops to suppress Klan; but Klan succeeded in many areas after troops left.
🔵 Period 5
99. Compromise of 1877
📖 Definition
Secret deal: Rutherford B. Hayes (Republican) gets presidency in exchange for withdrawing federal troops from South.
⚡ Causes
Disputed 1876 election (Hayes vs. Tilden); Southern Democrats agreed to accept Hayes if Reconstruction ended.
📌 Effects
Reconstruction ended. "Redeemers" (Southern Democrats) regained power. Jim Crow segregation began. Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) "separate but equal."
🟣 Period 6 (1865–1898)
100. Transcontinental Railroad (Completed 1869)
📖 Definition
Railroad connected East and West. Built by Union Pacific (Irish workers) and Central Pacific (Chinese workers). Met at Promontory Point, UT.
⚡ Causes
Pacific Railway Act (1862) land grants; desire to settle West and transport goods.
📌 Effects
Created national market; destroyed buffalo herds; enabled conquest of Native Americans; led to time zones (1883).
🟣 Period 6
101. Chinese Exclusion Act (1882)
📖 Definition
First federal law banning immigration based on race/nationality. Prohibited Chinese laborers and barred them from citizenship.
⚡ Causes
Anti-Chinese violence (Rock Springs massacre 1885); fear of job competition; nativism.
📌 Effects
Chinese population declined; set precedent for future immigration restrictions (National Origins Act 1924). Repealed 1943.
🟣 Period 6
102. Dawes Act (1887)
📖 Definition
Broke up tribal lands into individual plots (160 acres per family) to force assimilation. Surplus land sold to whites.
⚡ Causes
Desire to destroy tribal sovereignty; "Americanize" Natives; open land to settlers.
📌 Effects
Native Americans lost ~90 million acres of land; destroyed communal landholding; poverty increased; cultural disruption.
🟣 Period 6
103. Wounded Knee Massacre (1890)
📖 Definition
U.S. troops killed ~300 unarmed Lakota Sioux (mostly women/children) performing Ghost Dance. Last major conflict of Indian Wars.
⚡ Causes
Fear of Ghost Dance movement (believed would restore Native world); Sitting Bull killed days earlier.
📌 Effects
Ended Native armed resistance; frontier officially "closed." Symbol of U.S. brutality.
🟣 Period 6
104. Andrew Carnegie & Vertical Integration
📖 Definition
Carnegie Steel controlled every production stage (iron mines → steel mills → railroads). Used Gospel of Wealth (wealthy should give back through philanthropy).
⚡ Causes
Industrialization; demand for steel (railroads, skyscrapers).
📌 Effects
Made Carnegie one of richest men; gave away ~$350 million (libraries, Carnegie Hall, universities). Inspired philanthropy but also criticized as justifying inequality.
🟣 Period 6
105. John D. Rockefeller & Horizontal Integration
📖 Definition
Standard Oil bought out competitors to create monopoly. Used trusts (legal device to consolidate control).
⚡ Causes
Competition in oil refining; desire to control prices.
📌 Effects
Controlled 90% of U.S. oil refining; Sherman Antitrust Act (1890) eventually broke up Standard Oil (1911). "Robber baron" as archetype.
🟣 Period 6
106. Social Darwinism
📖 Definition
Applied "survival of the fittest" (Herbert Spencer) to society/business. Justified wealth inequality, opposed government regulation, defended laissez-faire.
⚡ Causes
Gilded Age inequality; influence of Darwin's evolution theory.
📌 Effects
Used by industrialists to oppose unions and welfare; also used to justify racism and imperialism.
🟣 Period 6
107. Knights of Labor (1869–1886)
📖 Definition
Early union open to all workers (skilled/unskilled, women, Black Americans). Sought 8-hour day, end of child labor, cooperative economy.
⚡ Causes
Labor exploitation in Gilded Age; lack of worker rights.
📌 Effects
Grew to 700,000 members; declined after Haymarket Riot (1886) – public blamed Knights for bombing. Replaced by AFL.
🟣 Period 6
108. Haymarket Riot (1886)
📖 Definition
Labor protest in Chicago for 8-hour day. Someone threw bomb at police; police fired into crowd. 7 police and 4 civilians killed.
⚡ Causes
Labor tensions; anarchist involvement.
📌 Effects
Public blamed unions (especially Knights of Labor); Knights collapsed; led to anti-union sentiment and executions of anarchists.
🟣 Period 6
109. American Federation of Labor (AFL) – Samuel Gompers
📖 Definition
Union focused only on skilled workers and "bread and butter" issues (wages, hours, safety). Avoided political radicalism.
⚡ Causes
Failure of Knights of Labor; need for more practical union.
📌 Effects
Became largest union (over 1 million by 1900); model for modern labor movement; excluded unskilled and women initially.
🟣 Period 6
110. Homestead Strike (1892)
📖 Definition
Violent strike at Carnegie Steel (Homestead, PA). Carnegie's manager Henry Frick hired Pinkerton agents; workers fought back. State militia crushed the union.
⚡ Causes
Wage cuts; union (Amalgamated Iron and Steel) refused to accept.
📌 Effects
Union destroyed at Homestead; set back steel unionization for decades. Showed government always sided with corporations.
🟣 Period 6
111. Pullman Strike (1894)
📖 Definition
Nationwide railroad strike against wage cuts (Pullman Palace Car Company). Led by Eugene V. Debs. Federal troops intervened via court injunction (Sherman Antitrust Act used against labor).
⚡ Causes
Pullman cut wages but not rent in company town; American Railway Union supported.
📌 Effects
Debs arrested; strike broken; Sherman Antitrust Act used to crush unions (not monopolies). Debs later became socialist.
🟣 Period 6
112. Social Gospel
📖 Definition
Protestant movement urging Christians to address societal ills (poverty, unsafe workplaces) as moral duty. Led to urban reform and settlement houses.
⚡ Causes
Industrial poverty; Second Great Awakening legacy.
📌 Effects
Settlement houses (Hull House – Jane Addams); YMCA; Salvation Army; influenced Progressive Era reforms.
🟣 Period 6
113. Populist Party (People's Party) – 1892
📖 Definition
Founded by farmers (Grange, Farmers' Alliances). Omaha Platform: direct election of senators (17th Am.), unlimited silver coinage, graduated income tax (16th Am.), government ownership of railroads, 8-hour day.
⚡ Causes
Economic hardship for farmers; railroad abuses; deflation (gold standard favored creditors).
📌 Effects
William Jennings Bryan (1896) "Cross of Gold" speech – lost to McKinley. Populist Party dissolved; many reforms adopted in Progressive Era.
🟣 Period 6
114. Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)
📖 Definition
Supreme Court ruled "separate but equal" constitutional. Upheld Jim Crow segregation for over 50 years.
⚡ Causes
Louisiana law requiring segregated railcars; Homer Plessy (1/8 Black) challenged.
📌 Effects
Legalized segregation (schools, public facilities). Not overturned until Brown v. Board (1954).
🟣 Period 6
115. Spanish-American War (1898)
📖 Definition
U.S. defeated Spain in 4 months. Treaty of Paris: U.S. got Guam, Puerto Rico, Philippines (paid $20 million); Cuba independent but under Platt Amendment (U.S. right to intervene).
⚡ Causes
Cuban independence struggle; yellow journalism (Hearst/Pulitzer); De Lôme Letter; USS Maine explosion ("Remember the Maine").
📌 Effects
U.S. emerged as world power; Philippine-American War (1899-1902); debate over imperialism (Anti-Imperialist League).
🟣 Period 6
116. Platt Amendment (1901)
📖 Definition
Added to Cuba's constitution. U.S. could intervene in Cuba unilaterally, Cuba couldn't sign treaties limiting independence; U.S. could lease Guantanamo Bay.
⚡ Causes
U.S. desire to control Cuba after Spanish-American War.
📌 Effects
Made Cuba a U.S. protectorate (not independent). Guantanamo still U.S. base.
🟣 Period 6
117. Open Door Policy (1899)
📖 Definition
Secretary of State John Hay proposed equal trading privileges for all nations in China. No formal spheres of influence.
⚡ Causes
Fear that European powers would carve up China and exclude U.S. trade.
📌 Effects
Preserved Chinese territorial integrity for U.S. interests; laid groundwork for later tensions with Japan.
⚪ Period 7 (1890–1945)
118. Theodore Roosevelt's "Square Deal" (1901–1909)
📖 Definition
Progressive domestic policy: Three C's – Control of corporations (trust-busting), Consumer protection, Conservation. Hepburn Act (1906) regulated railroads. Pure Food and Drug Act & Meat Inspection Act (1906).
⚡ Causes
Progressive reform pressure; Gilded Age abuses.
📌 Effects
Strengthened federal regulation; established conservation system (national parks, forests); set precedent for active presidency.
⚪ Period 7
119. Muckrakers
📖 Definition
Investigative journalists who exposed corruption, unsafe conditions, inequality. Examples: Ida Tarbell (Standard Oil), Upton Sinclair (The Jungle → Pure Food and Drug Act), Jacob Riis (tenements), Lincoln Steffens (city corruption).
⚡ Causes
Industrialization; corruption; public appetite for reform.
📌 Effects
Legislation (Meat Inspection, Pure Food, antitrust enforcement); public awareness.
⚪ Period 7
120. 16th Amendment (1913)
📖 Definition
Graduated federal income tax.
⚡ Causes
Need for revenue after tariff reductions; Populist/Progressive demand to tax wealthy.
📌 Effects
Major source of federal revenue; allowed redistribution through taxation.
⚪ Period 7
121. 17th Amendment (1913)
📖 Definition
Direct election of U.S. senators (previously chosen by state legislatures).
⚡ Causes
Populist demand to reduce corruption (state legislatures deadlocked or bribed).
📌 Effects
Senators more accountable to voters; reduced legislative corruption.
⚪ Period 7
122. Federal Reserve Act (1913)
📖 Definition
Created modern banking system – Federal Reserve Board, 12 regional banks, ability to set interest rates and control money supply.
⚡ Causes
Panic of 1907 (bank runs); need for lender of last resort.
📌 Effects
Stabilized banking system; still exists today.
⚪ Period 7
123. Clayton Antitrust Act (1914)
📖 Definition
Strengthened Sherman Act; exempted labor unions from antitrust laws; prohibited price discrimination, interlocking directorates.
⚡ Causes
Weakness of Sherman Act (used against unions, not trusts).
📌 Effects
Labor unions protected; effective trust-busting tool.
⚪ Period 7
124. Keating-Owen Act (1916)
📖 Definition
Outlawed child labor in manufacturing. Struck down by Supreme Court (Hammer v. Dagenhart, 1918).
⚡ Causes
Reformers concerned about child exploitation; muckraker photos.
📌 Effects
Child labor continued until Fair Labor Standards Act (1938). Showed limits of federal power before New Deal.
⚪ Period 7
125. 18th Amendment (1919) & Prohibition
📖 Definition
Outlawed alcohol (manufacture, sale, transportation). Enforced by Volstead Act. Repealed by 21st Amendment (1933).
⚡ Causes
Temperance movement; anti-immigrant sentiment.
📌 Effects
Rise of organized crime (Al Capone); speakeasies; increased lawlessness; failure of enforcement led to repeal.
⚪ Period 7
126. 19th Amendment (1920)
📖 Definition
Women's suffrage (right to vote).
⚡ Causes
Decades of women's rights activism (Seneca Falls 1848); women's war work (WWI).
📌 Effects
Doubled electorate; but many Black women remained disenfranchised in South until 1965.
⚪ Period 7
127. Lusitania (1915)
📖 Definition
British passenger liner sunk by German U-boat; 1,198 killed including 128 Americans.
⚡ Causes
German unrestricted submarine warfare; U.S. neutrality.
📌 Effects
Shifted American opinion against Germany; pushed U.S. closer to WWI entry.
⚪ Period 7
128. Zimmermann Telegram (1917)
📖 Definition
German telegram proposing alliance with Mexico: Mexico would get lost territory (TX, NM, AZ) if it joined Germany against U.S. Intercepted by Britain.
⚡ Causes
German desperation to keep U.S. out of war.
📌 Effects
Outraged Americans; contributed to U.S. declaration of war (April 1917).
⚪ Period 7
129. U.S. Entry into WWI (1917)
📖 Definition
Congress declared war on Germany (April 6, 1917). Wilson: "make world safe for democracy."
⚡ Causes
Unrestricted submarine warfare; Zimmermann Telegram; Russian Revolution.
📌 Effects
U.S. tipped balance to Allies; led to Wilson's Fourteen Points; U.S. emerged as world power.
⚪ Period 7
130. Fourteen Points & Treaty of Versailles (1919)
📖 Definition
Wilson's post-war plan: self-determination, freedom of seas, free trade, League of Nations. Treaty of Versailles included War Guilt Clause (Germany blamed, forced to pay reparations).
⚡ Causes
Wilson's idealism; Allies wanted punishment.
📌 Effects
Germany humiliated → WWII. Senate rejected treaty (feared League compromised sovereignty). U.S. never joined League.
⚪ Period 7
131. Great Migration (1915–1970)
📖 Definition
Movement of millions of Black Americans from South to northern/western cities (NYC, Chicago, Detroit, LA).
⚡ Causes
Escape Jim Crow, racial violence, sharecropping debt; industrial jobs in North (wartime labor shortage).
📌 Effects
Harlem Renaissance; race riots (Chicago 1919, Tulsa 1921); changed urban demographics.
⚪ Period 7
132. Red Scare & Palmer Raids (1919–1920)
📖 Definition
Anti-communist fear after Russian Revolution; anarchist bombings. Attorney General A. Mitchell Palmer arrested 6,000+ immigrants; hundreds deported without due process.
⚡ Causes
Fear of Bolshevik revolution spreading; labor strikes (1919).
📌 Effects
Violated civil liberties (due process); contributed to immigration restrictions (1924).
⚪ Period 7
133. Emergency Quota Act (1921) & National Origins Act (1924)
📖 Definition
Set low immigration quotas for Southern/Eastern Europeans (based on 1890 census – before mass immigration); virtually banned Asian immigration.
⚡ Causes
Nativism; Red Scare; KKK resurgence.
📌 Effects
Reduced immigration dramatically; favored NW Europeans; Chinese Exclusion Act until 1943.
⚪ Period 7
134. Sacco and Vanzetti (1927)
📖 Definition
Italian immigrant anarchists executed for murder (despite weak evidence). Worldwide protests.
⚡ Causes
Red Scare; anti-immigrant, anti-radical hysteria.
📌 Effects
Symbol of injustice; later exonerated (1977). Exposed flaws in justice system.
⚪ Period 7
135. Scopes Trial (1925)
📖 Definition
Tennessee teacher John Scopes arrested for teaching evolution (violated Butler Act). Clash between modernism and fundamentalism.
⚡ Causes
Religious fundamentalism vs. science; anti-evolution laws.
📌 Effects
Fundamentalists won but ridiculed in national media. Evolution still controversial today.
⚪ Period 7
136. Harlem Renaissance (1920s–1930s)
📖 Definition
Black artistic, literary, musical movement centered in Harlem, NYC. Figures: Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Louis Armstrong, Duke Ellington.
⚡ Causes
Great Migration; Black cultural pride; demand for civil rights.
📌 Effects
Increased visibility of Black culture; laid groundwork for Civil Rights Movement.
⚪ Period 7
137. Black Tuesday (October 29, 1929)
📖 Definition
Stock market crash. Values plummeted. Marked start of Great Depression.
⚡ Causes
Buying on margin; speculation; overproduction; weak banks.
📌 Effects
Bank failures (9,000 closed); unemployment 25%; Hoovervilles; global depression.
⚪ Period 7
138. Smoot-Hawley Tariff (1930)
📖 Definition
Raised tariffs to record levels (on 20,000+ products). Backfired – retaliatory tariffs collapsed world trade.
⚡ Causes
Protect U.S. farmers and industry; isolationism.
📌 Effects
World trade fell 65%; worsened Great Depression into global depression.
⚪ Period 7
139. Bonus Army (1932)
📖 Definition
17,000 WWI veterans marched on Washington for early bonus payment (due 1945). Hoover ordered Gen. MacArthur to forcibly evict them (tanks, cavalry, tear gas).
⚡ Causes
Economic desperation; veteran poverty.
📌 Effects
Hoover's popularity destroyed; FDR elected landslide; FDR sent wife to later meet veterans.
⚪ Period 7
140. New Deal (1933–1938)
📖 Definition
FDR's programs for Relief, Recovery, Reform. Key agencies: FDIC, SEC, CCC, WPA, TVA, AAA, Social Security Act (1935), Wagner Act, Fair Labor Standards Act (1938).
⚡ Causes
Great Depression; Hoover's failure to help individuals.
📌 Effects
Created modern safety net; realigned Democrats (New Deal Coalition). Did NOT end Depression (WWII did). Expanded federal power permanently.
⚪ Period 7
141. Social Security Act (1935)
📖 Definition
Created retirement pensions (funded by payroll tax), unemployment insurance, aid to dependent children (welfare).
⚡ Causes
Elderly poverty; unemployment widespread.
📌 Effects
Major expansion of federal responsibility; still exists. Excluded domestic/agricultural workers (mostly Black) until 1950s.
⚪ Period 7
142. Court Packing Plan (1937)
📖 Definition
FDR proposed adding 6 justices to Supreme Court (who struck down New Deal laws like NRA and AAA). Failed.
⚡ Causes
Supreme Court conservative opposition to New Deal.
📌 Effects
Damaged FDR's popularity; but Court began upholding New Deal laws ("switch in time that saved nine").
⚪ Period 7
143. Lend-Lease Act (March 1941)
📖 Definition
Allowed U.S. to sell, lease, or lend weapons to Allies (Britain, Soviet Union) without payment. "Arsenal of democracy."
⚡ Causes
Nazi victories in Europe; U.S. wanted to help without entering war.
📌 Effects
Ended neutrality; U.S. became "non-belligerent" supporter; Germany responded with submarine warfare.
⚪ Period 7
144. Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941)
📖 Definition
Japanese surprise attack on U.S. naval base in Hawaii. 2,400+ Americans killed; 8 battleships damaged/sunk; 300+ aircraft destroyed.
⚡ Causes
U.S. embargo on Japan (oil, steel) over Japan's invasion of China; Japan saw war as only option.
📌 Effects
U.S. declared war on Japan (Dec 8); Germany/Italy declared war on U.S. (Dec 11). U.S. entered WWII.
⚪ Period 7
145. Japanese Internment (Executive Order 9066, 1942)
📖 Definition
Forced relocation of 110,000+ Japanese Americans (2/3 U.S. citizens) to internment camps. Korematsu v. U.S. (1944) upheld as military necessity.
⚡ Causes
Pearl Harbor; racial prejudice; fear of espionage (no evidence).
📌 Effects
Loss of homes, businesses, liberty. Reparations paid 1988 ($20,000 each). Later criticized as racist.
⚪ Period 7
146. Double V Campaign (WWII)
📖 Definition
Black Americans' campaign for Victory abroad (against fascism) and Victory at home (against racism).
⚡ Causes
Segregation in military; discrimination in defense jobs.
📌 Effects
Executive Order 8802 (1941) banned discrimination in defense hiring (Fair Employment Practices Committee). Tuskegee Airmen, 442nd RCT proved valor. Paved way for civil rights movement.
⚪ Period 7
147. Rosie the Riveter
📖 Definition
Symbol of women in wartime factory jobs. By 1944, women made up 1/3 of civilian labor force.
⚡ Causes
Men went to war; labor shortage.
📌 Effects
Women gained economic independence; many lost jobs after war (expected to return home). But changed gender norms – contributed to later feminism.
⚪ Period 7
148. D-Day (June 6, 1944)
📖 Definition
Allied invasion of Normandy, France – largest amphibious invasion in history. Opened Western Front against Germany.
⚡ Causes
Tehran Conference (1943) agreement; needed to liberate Europe from Nazi control.
📌 Effects
Led to liberation of Paris (August 1944); Germany surrendered May 1945.
⚪ Period 7
149. Yalta and Potsdam Conferences (1945)
📖 Definition
Yalta: planned post-war Germany; Stalin promised free elections (broke promise); agreed to create UN. Potsdam: issued ultimatum to Japan; Truman learned atomic bomb test succeeded.
⚡ Causes
Need to coordinate post-war plans; defeat Japan.
📌 Effects
Division of Germany; Cold War tensions began; UN formed.
⚪ Period 7
150. Atomic Bombs (Hiroshima & Nagasaki – August 1945)
📖 Definition
U.S. dropped atomic bombs on Hiroshima (Aug 6) and Nagasaki (Aug 9). Japan surrendered August 15, 1945 (VJ Day).
⚡ Causes
Japan refused unconditional surrender; fear of costly invasion (estimated 1M U.S. casualties).
📌 Effects
Ended WWII; began nuclear age; U.S.-Soviet arms race; debate over necessity.
